Frequently Asked Questions

A reverse osmosis (RO) system is a water purification process that removes contaminants from water by using pressure to force water molecules through a semipermeable membrane. This process effectively reduces a wide range of impurities including heavy metals, salts, and other dissolved solids.

Filter replacement frequency can vary based on water quality and system usage, but as a general guideline, pre-filters should be replaced every 6 to 12 months, the RO membrane every 2 to 3 years, and post-filters annually. Always refer to the manufacturer's guidelines for your specific system.

Yes, reverse osmosis water is safe to drink. The RO process effectively removes many contaminants, resulting in clean and pure water. However, it also removes beneficial minerals, so some systems include an additional remineralization filter to add minerals back into the water.

A strange taste or odor in RO water can result from several factors, including clogged filters, bacterial growth, or improper storage. It's important to maintain the system properly by regularly replacing filters and sanitizing the storage tank to prevent these issues.

Traditional reverse osmosis systems typically waste about 4 gallons of water for every gallon of purified water produced. However, newer systems have become more efficient, with some models reducing the waste ratio significantly. Look for systems with a waste ratio of 1:1 or better for improved efficiency.
